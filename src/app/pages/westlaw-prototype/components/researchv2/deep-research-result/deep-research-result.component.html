Preliminary Response

The economic loss rule generally bars recovery in tort for purely economic losses arising from a breach of contract, but there are exceptions related to fraud. A claim resulting from a concealed defect likely would not be barred by the economic loss rule.

  1. The California Supreme Court in Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp.held that the economic loss rule does not bar claims for intentional misrepresentation or fraud in the performance of a contract when the fraud is independent of the breach of contract (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp., 34 Cal.4th 979 (2004)).
  2. InDhital v. Nissan North America, Inc., the court clarified that the economic loss rule does not bar tort damages in cases where the contract was fraudulently induced, including claims of intentional concealment(Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc., 84 Cal.App.5th 828 (2022)). This suggests that if your client's claim involves fraudulent inducement, it may not be barred by the economic loss rule.
  3. The court in In re Ford Motor Co. DPS6 Powershift Transmission Products Liability Litigation held that fraudulent omission claims are barred by the economic loss rule when the damages are purely economic and derive from the defendant's breach of warranty obligations (In re Ford Motor Co. DPS6 Powershift Transmission Products Liability Litigation, 483 F.Supp.3d 838 (2020)). This aligns with the general principle that the economic loss rule bars tort recovery for economic damages unless accompanied by physical harm or personal injury(In re Ford Motor Co. DPS6 Powershift Transmission Products Liability Litigation, 483 F.Supp.3d 838 (2020)).
  4. The court inFoster Poultry Farms v. Alkar-Rapidpak-MP Equipment, Inc. emphasized that to maintain a fraud claim based on the same factual allegations as a breach of contract claim, the plaintiff must show that the duty giving rise to tort liability is either completely independent of the contract or arises from conduct that is both intentional and intended to harm (Foster Poultry Farms v. Alkar-Rapidpak-MP Equipment, Inc., 868 F.Supp.2d 983 (2012)).

Summary

In California, the economic loss rule generally bars recovery of purely economic losses in tort actions, limiting recovery to contract remedies. There are exceptions, particularly in cases involving fraud.

The economic loss rule may not bar the fraud claim If your client can establish that:

  1. The manufacturer intentionally concealed a defect with the intent to defraud.
  2. The duty to disclose the defect involves a separate duty to disclose that arises independently of the contract.
  3. The concealment exposed your client to a risk of harm beyond what was reasonably contemplated by the parties when they entered the contract; and  
  4. your client relied on the omission to their detriment.

Analysis

Economic Loss Rule and Fraud Generally: The California Supreme Court in Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. held that the economic loss rule does not bar claims for intentional misrepresentation or fraud in the performance of a contract when the fraud is independent of the breach of contract (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp., 34 Cal.4th 979 (2004))[1].

Fraudulent Inducement:InDhital v. Nissan North America, Inc., the court clarified that the economic loss rule does not bar tort damages in cases where the contract was fraudulently induced, including claims of intentional concealment (Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc., 84 Cal.App.5th 828 (2022))[2]. This suggests that if your client's claim involves fraudulent inducement, it may not be barred by the economic loss rule.

Independent Duty: The court in Foster Poultry Farms v. Alkar-Rapidpak-MP Equipment, Inc.emphasized that to maintain a fraud claim based on the same factual allegations as a breach of contract claim, the plaintiff must show that the duty giving rise to tort liability is either completely independent of the contract or arises from conduct that is both intentional and intended to harm (Foster Poultry Farms v. Alkar-Rapidpak-MP Equipment, Inc., 868 F.Supp.2d 983 (2012))[3].

Fraudulent Omission: The court in In re Ford Motor Co. DPS6 Powershift Transmission Products Liability Litigation held that fraudulent omission claims are barred by the economic loss rule when the damages are purely economic and derive from the defendant's breach of warranty obligations (In re Ford Motor Co. DPS6 Powershift Transmission Products Liability Litigation, 483 F.Supp.3d 838 (2020))[4]. This aligns with the general principle that the economic loss rule bars tort recovery for economic damages unless accompanied by physical harm or personal injury (In re Ford Motor Co. DPS6 Powershift Transmission Products Liability Litigation, 483 F.Supp.3d 838 (2020))[4].

Fraudulent Concealment: In Rattagan v. Uber Technologies, Inc., 17 Cal.5th 1 (2024)[5] the court fount that a plaintiff may assert a cause of action for fraudulent concealment based on conduct occurring in the course of a contractual relationship, but only if two conditions are met: (1) the elements of the claim can be established independently of the parties’ contractual rights and obligations and (2) the tortious conduct exposes the plaintiff to a risk of harm beyond the reasonable contemplation of the parties when they entered into the contract.

Unusual situations

Non-contractual application: The economic loss rule most generally applies to suits between the parties to a contract. But as observed in Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, 12 Cal.5th 905, 922[6], a version of the doctrine may also apply in cases involving parties not in contractual privity, such as when a plaintiff seeks to impose liability in an indeterminate amount for an indeterminate time to an indeterminate class. See Rattagan v. Uber Techs., Inc., 17 Cal. 5th 1, 19, 553 P.3d 1213, 1223 (2024)[5]

Jurisdictional differences

  1. Florida Courts: Florida courts generally recognize a fraud exception to the economic loss rule. In Florida, if a party can prove that fraud occurred, they may be able to pursue tort claims even if the losses are purely economic and related to a contract.
  2. California Courts: California courts also recognize a fraud exception to the economic loss rule. They allow claims for fraud in the inducement of a contract, meaning if a party was fraudulently led into entering a contract, they can seek tort remedies.
  3. New York Courts: New York takes a narrower approach. The fraud exception is recognized, but the fraud must be "extraneous" to the contract. This means the fraudulent behavior must be separate from the contract terms themselves, not just a breach of contract disguised as fraud.
  4. Illinois Courts: Illinois courts generally do not recognize a broad fraud exception to the economic loss rule. The rule is applied strictly to maintain the boundary between contract and tort law. However, exceptions may exist if the fraud involves a duty independent of the contract.
  5. Texas Courts: Texas courts allow for a fraud exception but emphasize the need for a clear distinction between a tort duty and a contractual obligation. The fraud must be independent of the contract terms to pursue a tort claim.
  6. Michigan Courts: Michigan courts have a more restrictive view, often limiting the fraud exception. The focus is on whether the fraud is separate from the contract obligations, similar to New York's approach.

Some states do not apply the ELR or have exceptions to it, including Oklahoma and Tennessee (where the ELR applies only to products liability cases). Texas (where the courts have prohibited the application of the ELR as a defense to various statutory claims) and Colorado (where the courts have ruled the Colorado Legislature has the power to create causes of action that trump the ELR).
